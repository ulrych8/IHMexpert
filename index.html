We do not always invest in improving our interaction strategies. 
We tend to prefer familiar but suboptimal in- teractions, such as
"point-and-click" in a menu, instead of learning new interactions, 
such as shortcuts or gestural commands.
            
Time discounting theories from economics would describe this tendency
as our preference to favor an immediate reward (i.e. rapidly deliver 
our task now), over a long term reward (i.e. to learn a new method in 
order to improve this task at a future time).
            
In this paper we transpose and test an economic time discounting model
to a human-computer interaction context. Our model attempts to understand
how the choice of users to transition from novice to expert behavior 
evolves over time.
